Every family has its own story. In this interview, parents tell us how they introduced English audio stories at home and what changed in family dynamics and their children's motivation.

## Before: screens were in charge

Laura and Miguel have two children, aged 6 and 9. Before they started with audio stories, evenings at home were a tug-of-war: homework, tablet, TV, and the feeling that they weren’t really sharing something that brought them together. “We wanted English to be part of their day without it feeling like another chore,” Laura says. “But we didn’t know where to start without it sounding like school.”

A friend told them about personalized audio stories: tales in English with their kids’ names, their interests, and even their pet. They decided to try one night, without high expectations.

## The first night everything changed

Miguel remembers it clearly: “We played the story in the living room. Within five minutes both were quiet, listening. When they heard their names in the story, they looked at each other and burst out laughing. That was already magic.” That same night, for the first time in months, the older one asked for “another story” instead of “one more episode” on the tablet.

From then on, audio stories became part of the routine. Not as homework, but as a special moment: after bath time, with the lights dimmed, everyone in the same room. “It’s become our calm time before bed,” Laura says. “And without you realising, they’re listening to English, asking questions, laughing at the characters.”

## What actually changed at home

- **Bedtime** is no longer a battle. They have a ritual: pyjamas, story in English, lights off. The kids associate the audio with “time to wind down”.
- **English** stopped being “the difficult subject”. They hear it in context, with emotion and curiosity. At school they’ve noticed the children participate more and are less afraid of making mistakes.
- **Sibling bond** improved. They share favourite characters, tell each other what they liked about the story, and sometimes invent different endings.
- **The parents** got back the feeling of doing something good for their kids without lecturing. “It’s not ‘study English’, it’s ‘let’s listen to today’s adventure’,” Miguel sums up.

## One tip for other families

Laura is clear: “You don’t need to be bilingual or have loads of time. With fifteen minutes a day, a story that hooks them, and a bit of consistency, the change is real.” They recommend choosing stories that fit each child’s age and interests and not rushing: “At first they might not understand everything. What matters is that they enjoy it and that their ear gets used to it.”

If you’d like English to come into your home in a natural, family way too, personalized audio stories are a great place to start. Every family has its own story; this is Laura, Miguel and their children’s, and it might inspire yours.
